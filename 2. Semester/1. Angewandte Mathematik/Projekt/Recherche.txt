While the mean (average) value of $V$ only tells you the total amount of chemical on the board, the $L_2$-norm tells you how that chemical is distributed. The contrast of concentraion across the whole grid.

Because the $L_2$-norm formula squares the values before summing them, the math heavily rewards those intense peaks in Scenario B. Squaring $0.5$ yields $0.25$ (a massive contribution per pixel), whereas squaring $0.02$ yields a microscopic $0.0004$.

Why random seeds work as fix for spatial limitation: symmetry breaking and spatial scale. 

Pearson: 

He initialized a $256 \times 256$ grid to the trivial state ($U=1.0, V=0.0$).
He perturbed a $20 \times 20$ pixel square in the exact center to $U = 0.5$ and $V = 0.25$.Crucially: 
He then added $\pm 1\%$ random noise to this central patch.

We chose the random seeds, because it takes a long time to evolve with a central patch with random noise.

Mitosis: We have a spot:

If the parameters ($F$ and $k$) favor growth, the spot begins to expand outward because the activator $V$ diffuses into the surrounding fuel-rich territory.However, as the spot grows wider, a mathematical crisis occurs at its exact center:

-The perimeter of the spot is expanding and easily devouring fresh fuel from the outside.
-The center of the spot is now too far away from the fresh fuel supply. Because $U$ is being consumed rapidly on the outskirts, it cannot diffuse deeply enough into the core of the spot before getting eaten.
-The center of the spot literally starves. The concentration of $V$ drops in the middle, flattening the circular peak into a hollow ring or a "peanut" shape.

Once the center of the spot starves completely, $V$ drops to $0.0$ at the core, and $U$ rushes back in to fill the void. This cuts the peanut shape directly down the middle.

Diffusion Rate of U must be higher than V and feed rate can not be too high, since the center must starve. Feed rate cannot be too low or spots cannot grow. 

Mitosis happens in that perfect "sweet spot" where the center starves just enough to break the spot apart.

Laplacian operator: is fundamentally a comparison operator

We want to find the difference between our center pixel ($q_{i,j}$) and each of its four neighbors:

-Right comparison: $(q_{i, j+1} - q_{i,j})$
-Left comparison: $(q_{i, j-1} - q_{i,j})$
-Bottom comparison: $(q_{i+1, j} - q_{i,j})$
-Top comparison: $(q_{i-1, j} - q_{i,j})$

If we want to know the net flow of chemical into or out of our center pixel, we simply add all four of these comparisons together:

$$\text{Net Flow} = (q_{i, j+1} - q_{i,j}) + (q_{i, j-1} - q_{i,j}) + (q_{i+1, j} - q_{i,j}) + (q_{i-1, j} - q_{i,j})$$
Now, group the like terms. We have one of each neighbor, and we are subtracting the center $q_{i,j}$ exactly four times:
$$\text{Net Flow} = q_{i, j+1} + q_{i, j-1} + q_{i+1, j} + q_{i-1, j} - 4q_{i,j}$$

-If a pixel has more chemical than its neighbors, the Laplacian is negative (chemical flows out).
-If a pixel has less chemical than its neighbors, the Laplacian is positive (chemical flows in).

Reaction Term UV^2: U x V x V -> reaction = U * (V**2)

Feed (F) and kill (k): 
For U: +F(1-U)
For V: -(F+k)V

There are 3 forces acting on a chemical at any given pixel:
1. Net-Flow: Laplacian Operator.
2. Chemical-Reactions: UV^2
3. Feed-/Kill-Rate

┌────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
│ 1. Calculate Net Flow (Laplacian stencil)              				  │
│    How much U and V wants to diffuse in/out of pixel?  				  │
└───────────────────┬────────────────────────────────────┘
                    ▼
┌────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
│ 2. Calculate Chemistry (Reaction & Feed/Kill)                       	  |
│    How much U and V is being created or destroyed?                 	  |
└───────────────────┬────────────────────────────────────┘
                    ▼
┌────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
│ 3. Combine Forces (Find dU/dt and dV/dt)               				  │
│    Rate of Change = Diffusion + Reaction + Feed/Kill   				  │
└───────────────────┬────────────────────────────────────┘
                    ▼
┌────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
│ 4. Euler Step (U += dt * dU_dt)                        				  │
│    Project the new pixel values 1.0 time-step forward. 				  │
└────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘

A time step is the temporal resolution fo the simulation. It is the jump the simulation takes into the future on every single calculation. So the difference in accuracy of the calculation. The smaller the time step the closer the calculation to the true value.

The time step \Delta t is the temporal resolution
The spatial step \Delta x is the spatial resolution of the grid: Physical distance between the centers of any two adjacent pixels. In the real world it might be 0.1mm. Here we define the distance from pixel to pixel as 1 grid unit. Makes tracking easy.

  [Time Step 0]  --->  We start with current concentrations: U = 0.50, V = 0.25
        │
        ▼
  [Step 1: Space] --->  4-Point Stencil: "My neighbors have more U than me, so 
                        U is diffusing in. My neighbors have less V, so V is diffusing out."
        │
        ▼
  [Step 2: Chem]  --->  Reaction & Feed/Kill: "U and V are colliding here to make more V, 
                        while the feed rate replenishes U and the kill rate drains V."
        │
        ▼
  [Step 3: Rates] --->  Combine them: we sum the physical forces (Diffusion + Reaction + Feed/Kill).
                        This gives us our exact instantaneous rates: dU_dt and dV_dt.
        │
        ▼
  [Step 4: Euler] --->  Apply the step: New Concentration = Old Concentration + (1.0 * Rate of Change).
        │
        ▼
  [Time Step 1]  --->  The pixel instantly updates to its new values: U = 0.48, V = 0.27.

The visuals do not update for every \Delta t = 1.0, but instead the 4 step math is run 30 times in a row, jumping 30 steps into the future and then draws the new frame on the screen. So every frame is 30 time steps.
